Is Sicily Safe for Tourists? An Honest Local Guide

Last updated: May 2026
Short answer: Yes — Sicily is safe for tourists. Millions of visitors travel here every year without incident. Like any destination, a little local knowledge goes a long way.

It is one of the most Googled questions about the island, and understandably so. Sicily carries a weight of reputation — partly earned, mostly myth — that can make first-time visitors hesitant. We live and work here. We host guests from across the world. And we can tell you clearly: Sicily is a safe destination for tourists, including solo travellers, families and couples visiting for the first time.

This guide is honest. We will tell you where to be relaxed, where to pay more attention, and how to make the most of your visit without anxiety getting in the way.

How Sicily Compares to Other Destinations

Sicily consistently ranks as one of the safer regions in southern Europe for tourists. Violent crime against visitors is extremely rare. When compared to popular Mediterranean destinations such as Barcelona, Naples or certain areas of Rome and Marseille, Palermo and the rest of Sicily come out favourably. Pickpocketing on Barcelona's Las Ramblas, for example, is a far more common problem for tourists than anything you are likely to encounter in Palermo's historic centre.

Italy's own crime statistics place Sicily in the middle range nationally — neither the safest nor the most problematic region. What the statistics do not capture is how welcoming the culture is. Sicilians are famously hospitable. Strangers are treated as guests. This social fabric makes a genuine difference to how safe and at ease you feel day to day.

What to Be Aware Of

We want to be straightforward rather than dismissive. There are sensible precautions worth taking — the same ones you would apply in any busy city.

Pickpocketing in Markets

Palermo's street markets — Ballarò, Vucciria and Capo — are spectacular and absolutely worth visiting. They are also crowded, noisy and exactly the kind of environment where opportunistic pickpockets operate. Keep your phone in a front pocket or a zipped bag. Do not carry your passport unnecessarily. Wear a bag across your body rather than hanging off one shoulder. These simple steps are enough for the vast majority of visitors to have a completely trouble-free experience.

Unofficial Taxis

At Palermo's Falcone-Borsellino Airport and outside the central train station, unofficial drivers sometimes approach travellers. Always use the official white taxis with the city emblem, or book a pre-arranged transfer. Ride-hailing apps work well in Palermo and are a convenient alternative. Our concierge team at Le Sicilien always arranges trusted transfers for guests — it is one of those small details that removes a potential headache before it starts.

Driving and Traffic

Palermo's traffic is lively. Scooters weave between lanes, parking is creative and pedestrian crossings are treated as suggestions rather than rules. As a pedestrian, make eye contact with drivers before you step out. As a driver, stay calm, be assertive and do not expect orderly lane discipline. Sicilian driving is not dangerous so much as it is expressive — once you adjust your expectations, it becomes part of the experience.

Palermo: Which Areas Are Comfortable, Which Deserve More Attention

Palermo is a layered city and it helps to understand the geography before you arrive.

The Historic Centre

The four historic mandamenti — Albergheria, Capo, Vucciria and Kalsa — have all seen significant regeneration in recent years. Streets that felt neglected a decade ago now host boutique restaurants, wine bars and cultural spaces. Walking here during the day is entirely comfortable. At night, stick to the lit, busier streets and you will be fine. Wandering into isolated side alleys late at night is unnecessary and best avoided, as it would be in any European city. The area around Via Maqueda and the Quattro Canti is lively well into the evening and perfectly pleasant.

Mondello

Mondello, the coastal village just 11 kilometres from central Palermo, is one of the most relaxed and family-friendly areas in western Sicily. The beach, the Art Nouveau architecture and the seafood restaurants make it a genuinely lovely place to base yourself. Crime here is minimal. It feels closer to a quiet seaside resort than an urban environment, and many of our guests choose it specifically for the slower pace and the sea air. Our Mondello villa is particularly popular with families for exactly this reason.

Peripheral Neighbourhoods

Areas such as Zen and parts of the Borgonuovo district on the northern outskirts of Palermo are residential neighbourhoods with limited tourist infrastructure. There is no particular reason for a visitor to spend time there, and they are best left off the itinerary. This is not a dramatic warning — it is simply the practical observation that these areas offer nothing a tourist needs and carry the low-level tension found in any city's more economically marginalised outskirts.

Day Trips and the Rest of the Island

Beyond Palermo, Sicily is remarkably easygoing from a safety perspective. Cefalù, Taormina, Agrigento and the Valley of the Temples, the Aeolian Islands, Trapani and the salt pans — all are places where the biggest concern is sunburn rather than security. Small towns across the interior of the island are among the most tranquil environments in Europe. Locals will often go out of their way to help a confused tourist. Do not let an outdated reputation put you off exploring widely and slowly.
