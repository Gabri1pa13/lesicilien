Do I need a licence to rent my property in Palermo?

Last updated: April 1, 2026
Direct Answer: Yes. If you think you can just "put it on Airbnb" and hope for the best, you are inviting a 2,000 to 8,000 euro fine into your life.

I get asked this every week by people who have just bought a renovator's dream in the Kalsa or a Liberty-style apartment in Politeama. The "informal" days of Sicilian renting—where money changed hands in dark hallways and nobody asked for a passport—are dead and buried. Today, the Italian tax office (Agenzia delle Entrate) and the local police use algorithms to cross-reference portal listings with tax filings. If you don't have a licence, they will find you.

But let's clarify the terminology. In Italy, we don't usually call it a "licence" in the way you'd get a driver's licence. It is a series of registrations and declarations that make your property "compliant." In Palermo, this means navigating both Regional (Sicilian) and National (Italian) laws.

The CIR and the CIN: Your New Best Friends

The most important acronym you need to know is CIR (Codice Identificativo Regionale). In Sicily, every property used for tourism must have this code. You apply for it through the Osservatorio Turistico Regionale. Without this code, you cannot legally advertise your property. Airbnb and Booking.com will eventually block your listing if you don't input a valid CIR.

As if one code wasn't enough, 2024 saw the full rollout of the CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale). This is a federal requirement aimed at unifying the tourist database. If you have a CIR, you must now also claim your CIN through the national BDRS portal. If you are caught advertising without a visible CIN, the fines are brutal. This isn't a suggestion; it is a mandatory requirement for anyone wanting to rent even for a single night.

Locazione Turistica vs. Casa Vacanze

This is where most people get confused. Are you a Locazione Turistica (Tourist Rental) or a Casa Vacanze (Holiday Home)?

For most individual owners, Locazione Turistica is the path of least resistance. You are simply renting four walls and furniture. You cannot provide "extra" services like breakfast, daily cleaning while the guest is there, or organized tours. If you start offering those, you are technically a business and must register a Casa Vacanze, which requires a SCIA (Segnalazione Certificata di Inizio Attività) filed with the SUAP of the City of Palermo. This involves much stricter building codes and commercial tax rates.

In Palermo, if your apartment is in a historic building, getting a SCIA can be a nightmare because you might need to prove the property meets modern accessibility standards or has specific commercial designations. Stick to Locazione Turistica unless you are planning to run a full-blown boutique hotel.

The Questura: Reporting Your Guests

Even with your CIR and CIN in hand, you aren't done. Every guest that walks through your door must be reported to the Questura di Palermo via the Alloggiati Web portal. You have 24 hours to do this (or immediately if the stay is less than 24 hours). This is a national security requirement. Failing to report a guest is a criminal offense in Italy, not just an administrative one. You need to collect their passport or ID details at check-in—no exceptions.

Safety Requirements: No More "Creative" Wiring

Recent changes in Italian law (Legge 191/2023) have made certain safety features mandatory for all tourist rentals. You must have:

  • A functioning Carbon Monoxide (CO) detector in every room with a gas appliance (like your boiler or stove).
  • Fire extinguishers placed in accessible locations.
  • Gas and smoke detectors.

In Palermo, many old apartments have "creative" electrical systems and gas boilers tucked away in cupboards. If you want a licence, your systems must be certified (DI.RI. or DI.CO.). If a guest gets injured because of a non-compliant electrical system, your insurance will walk away faster than a Sicilian scooter in traffic.

The Tourist Tax (Tassa di Soggiorno)

Palermo is a city that needs its tourist revenue to maintain its monuments. As an owner, you are an "agent of the state" for the tourist tax. You must collect it from guests (usually between 1.50 and 3.00 euros per person per night, depending on the type of accommodation) and pay it to the municipality every quarter. You also need to file a yearly declaration (Modello 21). It is tedious, but again, the digital trail makes it impossible to hide.

Why Compliance is Actually Good for You

It sounds like a lot of work, and it is. But here is the secret: being fully compliant is your best marketing tool. Discerning guests—the ones who pay 300 euros a night for a luxury stay—look for the CIR/CIN in the listing. They want to know they are staying in a safe, legal property. Moreover, if you have a dispute with a guest or a neighbor, being 100% legal gives you the high ground. In Palermo, neighbors can be... vocal. If they know you are operating a legal rental, they are much less likely to call the authorities over a bit of late-night suitcase noise.
