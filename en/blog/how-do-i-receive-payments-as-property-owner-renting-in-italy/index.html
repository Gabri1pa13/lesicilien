How do I receive payments as a property owner renting in Italy?

Last updated: April 1, 2026
The Bottom Line: Money in Sicily moves differently. If you are waiting for a paper check or expecting a guest to leave an envelope of cash on the kitchen table, you are living in the 1990s.

You have the house, you have the furniture, and you have the CIR code. Now you want to see the euros in your bank account. "How do I receive payments as a property owner renting in Italy?" sounds like a simple question, but in the land of complex tax laws and digital gateways, the answer has layers. You are dealing with platforms, payment processors, and the ever-watchful Agenzia delle Entrate.

I have seen owners lose 20% of their revenue simply because they chose the wrong payout method or didn't understand how tax withholding works in Italy. In Palermo, we like to say i conti si fanno alla fine (the accounts are settled at the end). To make sure your "end" is profitable, you need a strategy.

Platform Payouts: The Big Two

Most owners start with Airbnb and Booking.com. They handle the money differently. Airbnb is the easiest: they collect the money from the guest, take their commission, and send you the rest (minus any taxes they might be forced to withhold) about 24 hours after the guest checks in. It is clean, predictable, and rarely fails.

Booking.com is more "Sicilian" in its complexity. Depending on how you set up your account, you might be responsible for collecting the money yourself. This means you need a virtual terminal or a gateway like Stripe. If you aren't careful, you might end up with a guest at your door who has "already paid" Booking.com, but the money is actually sitting in a virtual credit card that you need to manually charge. If you don't know how to do this, that money can expire. Professional managers use integrated channel managers to automate this, ensuring no cent is left on the table.

The Holy Grail: Direct Bookings

If you want to maximize your ROI, you need direct bookings. Why give 15-20% to a platform in San Francisco when you can keep it? But direct bookings mean you are the bank. You need a secure way to take credit card payments. We use Stripe for almost all our owners. It is reliable, handles currency conversion (great for your American or British guests), and integrates perfectly with professional management software.

Direct bookings also require a contract. In Italy, even for a three-day stay, you should have a signed contratto di locazione turistica. It protects you, and it makes the payment "official" in the eyes of the bank. If a bank sees thousands of euros hitting your account without a clear source, they might freeze it under anti-money laundering laws. Having a contract and a professional invoice ready is your insurance policy.

The 21% Question: Cedolare Secca and Withholding

This is the part that usually makes owners' heads spin. In Italy, rental income is often taxed at a flat rate of 21% (Cedolare Secca) for the first property. Starting in 2024, if you rent out more than one property, that rate jumps to 26% for the subsequent ones.

Since 2023, platforms like Airbnb are legally required to act as "tax substitutes." This means they might withhold 21% of your payout and pay it directly to the Italian government on your behalf. You then get a Certificazione Unica (CU) at the end of the year to show you've paid. If you live abroad, this can actually be a blessing—it saves you from having to file a complex tax return for that specific income. But if you are managing the payments yourself through Stripe or bank transfers, you are responsible for setting that 21% aside for your tax bill. Don't spend it on a new kitchen before you've settled with the taxman.

Security Deposits: Avoiding the "Damage" Drama

How do you handle the security deposit? In the old days, owners would take 200 euros in cash at check-in and give it back at check-out. This is a terrible idea for luxury rentals. It starts the guest's stay with a moment of distrust, and it forces you to have a potentially awkward confrontation if you find a scratch on your marble table at 6 AM during check-out.

The modern way is a "pre-authorization" on a credit card. You don't actually take the money; you just "freeze" it. If everything is fine, the freeze melts away. If there is damage, you have the leverage to make a claim. This is how we handle it at Le Sicilien—it is professional, invisible to the guest if no damage occurs, and legally sound.

Do You Need a Local Italian Bank Account?

Not necessarily. With services like Wise or Revolut, you can have an IBAN that works perfectly in Italy without the soul-crushing experience of trying to open a traditional account at a local branch in Palermo. Most Italian banks will want to see residency papers and a mountain of documents. A professional property manager can receive the funds into a dedicated escrow account and then transfer your net profit to any account in the world.

The Role of the Property Manager in the Money Flow

A good manager is also an accountant. Every month, you should receive a statement that looks like this:

  • Gross Revenue: What the guests paid.
  • Channel Fees: What Airbnb/Booking took.
  • Management Fee: What the manager earned.
  • Operational Costs: Cleaning, laundry, that broken lightbulb in the hall.
  • Tourist Tax: Collected and ready for the city.
  • Net Payout: What hits your bank account.

If your manager's reporting is a messy WhatsApp message or a handwritten note, find a new manager. In the luxury segment, financial precision is just as important as the quality of the linens.

Receiving payments in Italy is about building a system. Once the pipes are laid, the money flows. But if you try to "improvise" your financial setup, you will eventually find a leak that is very expensive to fix. Treat your Palermo rental like the business it is, and the rewards—both financial and personal—will follow.
